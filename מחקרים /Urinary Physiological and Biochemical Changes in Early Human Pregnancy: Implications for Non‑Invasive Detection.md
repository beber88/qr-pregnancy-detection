# Urinary Physiological and Biochemical Changes in Early Human Pregnancy: Implications for Non‑Invasive Detection

## 1. Overview and scope

Human pregnancy induces a dense set of endocrine, metabolic, renal, and urogenital changes that are reflected in urine from the peri‑implantation period through the first trimester. Many of these changes involve chromophoric molecules, colloids, or particulates that could, in principle, alter urine color, turbidity, foam, or sediment and thus be detectable in smartphone images.[^1][^2][^3]
This briefing synthesizes the literature on urinary changes from conception through 12–13 weeks’ gestation, emphasizing visually or colorimetrically accessible features and documenting molecular mechanisms, quantitative ranges, and key confounders.

## 2. hCG: molecular forms, kinetics, and assay behavior

### 2.1 Molecular structure and isoforms

Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) is a heterodimeric glycoprotein hormone composed of a common α‑subunit (shared with LH, FSH, and TSH) and a hormone‑specific β‑subunit; the intact non‑nicked heterodimer has a molecular mass of about 36–37 kDa with ~30% carbohydrate content.[^4][^3][^5]
Multiple isoforms relevant to urine include:

- **Intact hCG**: full α/β heterodimer, initially the dominant circulating and urinary species in very early pregnancy; principal target of most qualitative urine tests.[^5][^4]
- **Free β‑subunit (hCGβ)**: produced by trophoblast and by metabolic cleavage of intact hCG; subject to further proteolysis.[^6][^4][^5]
- **Hyperglycosylated hCG (h‑hCG)**: an hCG variant with larger and more branched O‑linked oligosaccharides, produced by invasive cytotrophoblasts; it is the principal hCG‑related molecule in the first week after implantation and around the time of the missed menses.[^7][^4]
- **β‑core fragment (hCGβcf / β‑C‑hCG)**: a ~10 kDa degradation product of the β‑subunit produced by macrophage and renal proteases; it is the major immunoreactive hCG‑related species in urine beyond about 5–6 weeks post‑conception.[^8][^4][^5]

Pregnancy urine therefore contains a mixture of intact hCG, nicked hCG, free α, free β, hyperglycosylated hCG, and β‑core fragment; the relative proportions shift with gestational age.[^9][^8][^5]

### 2.2 Urinary hCG isoform patterns over gestation

Daily first‑morning urine studies in naturally conceived pregnancies show that:

- Intact hCG and free β are the predominant urinary forms during the first 4–5 weeks post‑conception.
- β‑core fragment emerges later and becomes the predominant urinary hCG‑related analyte in the 5th week after conception and thereafter.[^8][^9][^5]
- The molar ratio β‑core:intact hCG in spot pregnancy urines is consistently >1 from the late first trimester onward.[^8]

A large cross‑sectional series of 741 pregnant women (6–41 weeks) found that urinary β‑core fragment concentrations increase in parallel with intact hCG but at higher molar ratios, peaking between 8–15 weeks and declining by 20–29 weeks.[^8]
Second‑trimester samples from 100 women demonstrated that β‑core concentrations often exceed intact hCG and are the major immunoreactive component in pregnancy urine.[^10]

Clearblue internal data and independent academic studies report that:

- Intact hCG appears in urine by about day 8 after ovulation, rising sharply over days 8–12.
- Free β‑hCG in urine is not consistently detectable until around day 21 post‑ovulation and remains at ~1:100 of intact hCG by molar ratio in early gestation.[^11][^9]
- β‑core fragment is rarely present before day ~19 but becomes the predominant form by approximately day 35 post‑ovulation.[^12][^9]

### 2.3 Urinary hCG concentration kinetics (peri‑conception through first trimester)

Quantitative urinary hCG distributions are more variable than serum because of hydration and renal handling, but several data sets allow construction of approximate ranges.

#### 2.3.1 Daily peri‑implantation kinetics

Clearblue modeling of daily first‑morning urine in 524 conceptions showed a ~30‑fold mean increase in urinary hCG from days 8 to 9 after LH surge, a 5‑fold increase from days 9 to 10, nearly 3‑fold from days 10 to 11, and a doubling from days 11 to 12.[^13]
CDC’s NHANES urine pregnancy test method sheet summarizes compiled data indicating that typical urinary hCG concentrations are **~10–30 mIU/mL** between 7–10 days after implantation (about 3 weeks after the last menstrual period), rising to a peak later in the first trimester.[^2]

Small physiologic studies report mean urinary hCG around **0.9 mIU/mL** at 9 days post‑conception, with rapid daily increases thereafter, plateauing around 45 days post‑conception.[^14]

#### 2.3.2 Approximate urinary hCG ranges by gestational week

Because most reference ranges are proprietary or reported graphically, precise numeric tables are limited; however, combining public summaries gives approximate typical ranges for first‑morning urine (FMU) in viable singleton pregnancies.[^14][^13][^2][^5]

#### 2.3.3 Isoform‑specific urinary data

Intact hCG, free β, and β‑core fragment have been quantified in daily urine during the first six weeks post‑conception for 37 natural pregnancies. Key observations include:[^9]

- Intact hCG and free β appear first and show the largest day‑to‑day variation; intact hCG can be transiently undetectable even 10 days after initial detection in a viable pregnancy.[^9]
- β‑core fragment appears later (often after day ~19) but rises to become the predominant urinary form by week 5 post‑conception.[^12][^9]
- Combined measurement of all forms (intact + free β + β‑core) gives the smoothest exponential rise and most stable doubling time, making it optimal for quantitative modeling.[^9]

Gronowski et al. showed in a trimester‑spanning series that β‑core fragment concentrations frequently exceed intact hCG, and that urine samples with high β‑core can yield false‑negative results on devices whose antibodies are inhibited by β‑core binding (variant hook effect).[^10]

### 2.4 Detection thresholds of current urine hCG assays

Most over‑the‑counter (OTC) and point‑of‑care urine pregnancy tests are qualitative sandwich immunoassays targeting various epitopes on intact hCG and, variably, on free β and β‑core.[^4][^16][^5]

- Typical claimed analytical sensitivity (limit of detection) is **20–25 mIU/mL** of hCG in urine.[^15][^1][^16]
- CDC’s ICON® 25 hCG urine test has a stated sensitivity of **25 mIU/mL** and can detect pregnancy about **1 day after the first missed menses** under ideal conditions.[^15]
- StatPearls notes that qualitative urine tests generally detect hCG at **20–50 mIU/mL**, with some reports as low as 6.3–12.5 mIU/mL, typically corresponding to approximately 4 weeks post‑conception.[^1]

Empirical evaluations show that advertised analytical sensitivities often over‑state diagnostic sensitivity at very low concentrations; for two widely used POC urine devices, urinary hCG concentrations of **150–225 mIU/L** were required to achieve **99% sensitivity** around the time of the missed menses.[^16]

Hyperglycosylated hCG detection is highly assay‑dependent; a panel study of 14 laboratory and 18 home tests found that only 3 laboratory assays and 7/18 home tests adequately detected hyperglycosylated hCG, despite it being the principal hCG form in the week after the missed period.[^7]

### 2.5 Half‑life, clearance, and diurnal variation

Serum hCG has a terminal half‑life on the order of **24–36 hours** after trophoblast removal (e.g., postpartum or post‑evacuation), with more rapid distribution phases; similar kinetics likely govern urinary excretion, which reflects integrated serum levels and renal clearance.[^18][^3][^1]

- Urinary hCG immunoreactivity is susceptible to degradation during sample storage: at −20 °C, 20–100% loss of immunoreactivity occurred in 15/17 urine samples, with urea implicated in peptide degradation; stability is better at −80 °C or +4 °C.[^18]
- hCG and β‑core fragment are freely filtered by the glomerulus and excreted without reabsorption; creatinine‑normalized urinary hCG improves comparability between samples by partially correcting for urine concentration.[^8][^9]

Diurnal variation in urinary hCG per se has been less well characterized than for β‑core fragment and related analytes; however, several principles are relevant:

- First‑morning urine generally yields the highest hCG concentration due to overnight concentration and is therefore recommended for early testing.[^2][^5][^1]
- A study of urinary β‑core fragment diurnal variation in pregnancy found fluctuations over a 24‑hour period, but expressing β‑core relative to creatinine or total volume substantially reduced variability, indicating that much of the apparent diurnal pattern reflects fluid intake and urine flow rather than pulsatile secretion.[^19]
- An earlier study of β‑core diurnal variation in gynecologic oncology patients similarly concluded that concentration per unit volume varied more than excretion rate, again implicating urine volume as the main determinant.[^20][^21]

For a vision‑based detection system, this argues strongly for standardizing to first‑morning void and, where possible, estimating urine concentration (e.g., via color‑based surrogates of specific gravity) to contextualize apparent chromatic or turbidity changes.

### 2.6 Causes of false‑positive and false‑negative urine hCG

#### 2.6.1 False positives

Established causes of falsely positive urine hCG results include:

- **Analytical interference by heterophilic / human anti‑animal antibodies (HAMA)**: These antibodies can bridge capture and detection antibodies in immunoassays, producing spurious positive results in both serum and urine tests.[^22][^23]
- **Pituitary hCG**: Peri‑menopausal or post‑menopausal women may secrete low levels of hCG from the pituitary, leading to low‑positive serum and occasionally urine tests in the absence of pregnancy.[^3][^1]
- **Trophoblastic and nontrophoblastic tumors**: Choriocarcinoma, gestational trophoblastic disease, germ‑cell tumors, and some other malignancies secrete hCG or free β, detectable in urine.[^3][^1]
- **Recent pregnancy, abortion, or miscarriage**: hCG can persist for weeks after resolution of pregnancy, giving positive urine tests for up to ~4 weeks after medical abortion or incomplete miscarriage.[^24]
- **Exogenous hCG administration**: Fertility drugs and certain performance‑enhancing regimens containing hCG cause transient positive results.[^24]
- **Renal failure or contamination**: Severe proteinuria, hematuria, or pyuria may rarely interfere with chromatographic flow or antibody binding and produce indeterminate or misleading bands, although robust clinical data are sparse.[^25][^26]

These mechanisms can generate hCG‑positive urine without viable intrauterine pregnancy and would appear to a vision‑only system as true positives (since colorimetric strips are directly affected), but they do not typically induce gross visual changes in native urine.

#### 2.6.2 False negatives

Mechanisms for false‑negative urine hCG tests in confirmed pregnancy include:

- **Very early gestation** with urinary hCG below the device’s functional detection threshold (e.g., <20–50 mIU/mL).[^1][^15][^16]
- **Dilute urine** due to high fluid intake, reducing hCG below the assay’s cut‑off despite normal serum levels.[^5][^1]
- **High‑dose hook effect**: Extremely high hCG concentrations (often >500,000 mIU/mL) saturate both capture and detection antibodies, preventing sandwich formation and yielding a false‑negative test.[^17][^26][^25]
  - Variant hook effects may be driven particularly by β‑core fragment if antibodies recognize different epitopes, leading to competitive blocking.[^25][^10]
- **Assays that poorly detect hyperglycosylated hCG**: In very early pregnancy, hyperglycosylated hCG predominates; tests with low cross‑reactivity to this form may miss pregnancies around the time of missed menses.[^7]
- **Technical/user errors**: Incorrect timing, inadequate specimen volume, expired reagents, or improper storage can all yield false‑negatives.[^15][^16]

For AI‑based interpretation of test strips, some of these failure modes (e.g., hook effect) could be inferred from abnormal band patterns, but most are not accessible from native urine appearance alone.

## 3. Other hormones and metabolites in pregnancy urine

### 3.1 Estrogens: estriol and estrone‑3‑glucuronide

Estriol (E3) in late pregnancy is produced largely by the fetoplacental unit and excreted in urine mostly as glucuronide conjugates; historic fetal surveillance used 24‑hour urinary estriol excretion, which rises steadily across gestation.[^27][^28]

Daily early‑luteal monitoring studies demonstrate that conception cycles show higher urinary estrone‑3‑glucuronide (E1‑3G) beginning ~7 days after the LH peak compared with non‑conception cycles:[^29]

- In a classic study of six non‑pregnant and five conception cycles, women who conceived excreted **significantly higher E1‑3G** 7 days after the LH peak; pregnanediol‑3α‑glucuronide (PdG) and LH/hCG also rose by day 12.[^29]
- A large cohort of 347 fertile women providing daily urine showed that cycles culminating in clinical pregnancy had higher early‑luteal estrone conjugate (E1C) and progesterone metabolite PdG concentrations than non‑conception cycles.[^30]

In that cohort, daily creatinine‑normalized E1C during cycles with clinical pregnancy was on average **18–20 ng/mg creatinine** higher than in non‑conception cycles, and E1C <10th percentile (<30 ng/mg Cr) in early luteal phase was associated with significantly higher odds of early pregnancy loss.[^30]

These differences, however, are in the low‑nanogram range and are not visually apparent; they require immunoassay or mass spectrometry.[^31][^32]

### 3.2 Progesterone metabolites: pregnanediol‑3‑glucuronide

Pregnanediol‑3‑glucuronide (PdG) is the principal urinary metabolite of progesterone and reflects luteal function.
Conception cycles show sustained luteal PdG elevation compared with non‑conception cycles, and ongoing clinical pregnancy is associated with persistently higher PdG across the luteal phase.[^29][^30]

In frozen embryo transfer cycles, preliminary work suggests thresholds in urinary PdG that correlate with adequate serum progesterone for implantation and ongoing pregnancy, though exact numeric cut‑offs vary by protocol and analytical method.[^33]

From the perspective of visual detection, PdG and related steroids are colorless at physiologic concentrations; their main relevance is as potential analytes for enzymatic colorimetric strip chemistries rather than native urine color.

### 3.3 Placental lactogen (hPL) and relaxin

Human placental lactogen (hPL) is a placental peptide hormone with a serum half‑life of ~90 minutes; it is excreted unchanged in urine, and early studies demonstrated its measurability and potential for pregnancy dating based on rising concentrations.[^34][^35]
Serum hPL increases steadily from early pregnancy to term, with typical ranges of ~0.2–2.1 μg/mL in first trimester, 0.5–6.7 μg/mL in second trimester, and 4.5–12.8 μg/mL in third trimester, falling rapidly postpartum.[^35][^34]
Urinary hPL has been proposed as a dating and viability marker but is not used clinically and there is little modern quantitative urine data.

Relaxin in human pregnancy is produced predominantly by the corpus luteum; secretion from endometrium, decidua, and placenta appears to be paracrine and not reflected in systemic circulation.[^36]
Serum relaxin peaks in the first trimester and has been implicated in cervical remodeling and preterm birth risk when excessively elevated or absent.[^36]
Unlike in veterinary species, validated human urinary relaxin assays for routine use are lacking; published urinary relaxin work is largely in animals such as cats and dogs.[^37][^38][^39]
There is thus no robust quantitative human urinary relaxin profile across early pregnancy.

### 3.4 Broad steroid metabolite panels

LC–MS/MS analyses of spot urine from normal singleton pregnancies show gestation‑specific increases in a wide variety of glucocorticoid, mineralocorticoid, androgen, and estrogen metabolites, with reference intervals defined for each gestational window.[^32][^40]
These include tetrahydrocortisol, tetrahydrocortisone, cortisone and cortisol metabolites, 17‑hydroxyprogesterone derivatives, androsterone, etiocholanolone, and others.[^41][^32]

Although metabolically important, these steroids are present at micromolar or lower concentrations and are transparent in bulk solution; they do not produce macroscopic color or turbidity changes discernible on visual inspection, but again may be amenable to selective colorimetric reactions on impregnated pads.

## 4. Non‑hormonal compositional changes in pregnancy urine

### 4.1 Proteinuria: physiologic vs pathological

Normal pregnancy increases renal plasma flow and glomerular filtration rate, leading to a modest physiological increase in urinary protein excretion.[^42][^43]

- In non‑pregnant adults, 24‑hour urinary protein excretion is typically <150 mg/day.[^44][^42]
- In uncomplicated pregnancy, excretion up to **~200–250 mg/day** can be seen, and reference tables list 0–255 mg/24 h in second trimester and 0–254 mg/24 h in third trimester.[^42][^44]
- Current ACOG and nephrology definitions retain **≥300 mg/24 h** (or spot urine protein:creatinine ratio ≥0.3) as the diagnostic threshold for proteinuria in pregnancy, especially in the context of hypertension.[^45][^46][^47]

A contemporary review notes that using this threshold, isolated gestational proteinuria (without hypertension) may occur in up to 8% of pregnancies, whereas preeclampsia occurs in ~3–8%.[^47]
Dipstick screening has poor sensitivity and specificity and is not recommended for diagnosis.[^47][^42]

From a visual standpoint, **overt proteinuria** (e.g., ≥1+ to 2+ on dipstick or >300 mg/day) can increase foam persistence and contribute modestly to turbidity, particularly when accompanied by lipiduria or cellular elements.
However, physiologic proteinuria in otherwise healthy pregnancies is typically below the threshold for easily visible changes.

### 4.2 Glucosuria

Pregnancy lowers the renal threshold for glucose due to increased GFR and altered tubular reabsorption, so **mild glucosuria is common** even in normoglycemic pregnant women.[^48][^49]
In a strip‑based survey of 50 pregnant women, glucose was trace in 34% and positive in 16% despite most being clinically well.[^48]

Glucose at physiologic concentrations does not impart visible color or turbidity; however, heavy glucosuria (e.g., in uncontrolled diabetes) can slightly increase specific gravity and osmotic load, potentially altering refractive index and foam but not in a pregnancy‑specific way.

### 4.3 Ketones (especially in hyperemesis gravidarum)

Ketone bodies (acetoacetate, β‑hydroxybutyrate, acetone) appear in urine during starvation or carbohydrate restriction; in pregnancy they are commonly measured in the context of **hyperemesis gravidarum (HG)** or diabetic ketoacidosis.[^50][^51][^52]

- HG affects ~0.3–3% of pregnancies and is characterized by severe vomiting, weight loss ≥5%, dehydration, and often ketonuria.[^51][^50]
- Ketonuria (1+ or higher) has traditionally been a diagnostic criterion and triage marker for HG, although more recent systematic reviews question its correlation with severity.[^53][^52][^50]

Ketones themselves are colorless; standard dipsticks use nitroprusside reactions to generate a purple color.
Native urine in ketotic states may be more concentrated (darker yellow to amber) due to dehydration, but this is not specific to pregnancy or HG.[^54][^55]

### 4.4 Specific gravity and osmolality

Urine specific gravity (USG) reflects total solute concentration and correlates with osmolality.

- In general populations, normal USG ranges from **~1.005–1.030**, with values <1.010 reflecting dilute urine and >1.025–1.030 indicating concentrated urine or high solute excretion.[^56][^57][^58]
- Studies in pregnant women report distributions similar to non‑pregnant adults, e.g., among 30 pregnant women, USG values of 1.010, 1.015, 1.020, 1.025, and 1.030 occurred in 20%, 30%, 6.7%, 20%, and 23.3% of samples, respectively.[^59]

Hydration studies in pregnant and lactating women demonstrate that urine color (on an 8‑point scale) correlates strongly with 24‑hour urine osmolality and specific gravity, and that a color score ≥4 predicts inadequate fluid intake (Uosm ≥500 mOsm/kg).[^55][^54]
Thus, **urine concentration‑driven color changes are a major determinant of visible urine color in pregnancy, as in non‑pregnant adults**.

### 4.5 pH changes and lithogenicity

Normal urine pH ranges from about **4.5 to 8.0** in adults, determined by diet, systemic acid–base balance, and urinary ammonium excretion.[^60][^61]

Pregnancy is associated with:

- Respiratory alkalosis (from progesterone‑driven hyperventilation) and renal compensatory bicarbonate excretion.
- Increased risk of nephrolithiasis, especially calcium phosphate stones, attributed partly to **more alkaline urine** and gestational hypercalciuria.[^62][^63][^64][^65][^66]

Prospective metabolic stone studies in pregnancy report increased urine pH, hypercalciuria (~240 mg/day vs ~190 mg/day in non‑pregnant stone formers), and supersaturation of calcium oxalate and brushite comparable to non‑pregnant stone formers, without overt pathological crystalluria in most women.[^64][^65]

However, pH values in asymptomatic pregnant women largely remain within the normal 4.5–8.0 range; pH alone does not discriminate pregnancy status, and a prospective screen of 510 obstetric patients found that urine pH was not useful in detecting asymptomatic bacteriuria.[^67]

### 4.6 Crystalluria patterns

Pregnancy‑associated changes in calcium and phosphate handling and urinary alkalinization predispose to calcium phosphate and calcium oxalate supersaturation, but **pathological crystalluria is not universal**.[^66][^68][^64]
One stone‑metabolism study concluded that gestational hypercalciuria raises calcium oxalate and brushite supersaturation to levels seen in calcium stone formers, yet pregnant women do not commonly form stones, suggesting protective factors such as increased citrate or inhibitors of crystallization.[^65][^66]

Limited data explicitly quantify crystalluria in normal pregnancy, but small series in pregnant women with UTI show a range of crystal types (e.g., calcium oxalate) with weak correlation to pyuria and bacteriuria.[^69]
The presence of crystals increases microscopic sediment and may contribute modestly to turbidity but does not yield pregnancy‑specific visual signatures.

### 4.7 Leukocytes, nitrites, and UTI incidence

Pregnancy predisposes to urinary stasis, vesicoureteral reflux, and hydronephrosis, raising the risk of **urinary tract infection (UTI)**.[^49][^70]

- Asymptomatic bacteriuria occurs in **~2–13%** of pregnant women; symptomatic UTI (cystitis) in ~1–2%; overall, some guidelines quote ~8% of pregnancies affected by UTI.[^71][^70][^49]
- Dipstick leukocyte esterase has high sensitivity (~97%) but limited specificity for UTI because vaginal leukocytes can contaminate samples.[^72][^73][^74]
- Nitrites are highly specific (94–98%) but less sensitive because not all uropathogens reduce nitrate.[^73][^74][^72]

A cross‑sectional study of 56 pregnant women with asymptomatic bacteriuria found leukocyte esterase positivity in 48.2% and nitrite positivity in 58.9%; most had turbid urine (58.9%) and pH around 6.0.[^71]
Another study of 86 pregnant women vs 30 non‑pregnant controls found increased morphotic elements (leukocytes, bacteria) in urine with advancing pregnancy, especially in those with significant bacteriuria.[^75]

Clinically, UTI‑associated leukocyturia and bacteriuria produce turbidity, sediment, and occasionally visible hematuria.
However, these features reflect infection rather than pregnancy per se, and similar appearances occur in non‑pregnant women with UTI.

## 5. Visually observable urine changes in pregnancy

### 5.1 Color shifts: evidence vs folklore

Native urine color is determined primarily by concentration of urochrome (urobilin) and to a lesser extent by urobilinogen and other pigments; darker color corresponds to higher osmolality and specific gravity, whereas pale color indicates dilution.[^61][^54][^55][^60]

Hydration studies show that in pregnant and lactating women, urine color scores correlate with osmolality and specific gravity similarly to non‑pregnant controls; a 24‑hour urine color score ≥4 predicts Uosm ≥500 mOsm/kg, indicating inadequate fluid intake.[^54][^55]
Thus, **pregnancy does not introduce unique pigments into urine at visually detectable concentrations**; color shifts mainly reflect hydration status, dietary components, and supplements.

Common visually appreciable color modifiers relevant in pregnancy include:

- **Dehydration**: darker yellow to amber urine with higher USG; common in early pregnancy due to nausea and vomiting.[^76][^55][^54]
- **Prenatal vitamins (especially riboflavin/B2 and other B‑complex vitamins)**: bright or neon yellow to yellow‑green urine due to excretion of highly fluorescent riboflavin and related compounds.[^77][^78][^79][^80]
- **Food pigments**: beets, blackberries, rhubarb, and food dyes can transiently color urine red, pink, orange, or other hues; asparagus may give a greenish tinge and characteristic odor.[^81][^82][^83][^84]

Popular folklore claims (e.g., mixing urine with bleach or other household reagents to diagnose pregnancy) are unsupported by controlled studies; documented color changes from such mixtures reflect non‑specific chemical reactions with urea, creatinine, or ammonia rather than pregnancy‑specific constituents.
No peer‑reviewed evidence indicates that pregnancy alone produces macroscopic urine color shifts distinguishable from hydration/diet effects.

### 5.2 Turbidity

Turbidity (cloudiness) arises from suspended particles: leukocytes, bacteria, epithelial cells, crystals, mucus, lipids, and precipitated phosphates.[^85][^86][^60][^61]

Pregnancy‑related influences include:

- **Higher UTI incidence**: as noted, asymptomatic bacteriuria and cystitis are more common in pregnancy; turbidity, leukocyte esterase, and nitrites show strong associations with bacteriuria in pregnant women and are significant diagnostic indicators.[^70][^75][^49][^71]
- **Physiologic crystalluria and hypercalciuria**: increased urinary calcium, slightly more alkaline pH, and altered excretion of sulfate and other anions may promote transient precipitation of phosphates or calcium salts, contributing to mild turbidity in concentrated or alkaline urine.[^87][^62][^64][^65]

However, turbidity as a visual feature is **non‑specific**—equally produced by UTI, stone disease, heavy phosphaturia after meals, semen contamination, or vaginal secretions in non‑pregnant individuals.[^86][^85][^60]
There is no evidence for a pregnancy‑specific turbidity pattern independent of infection or concentration.

### 5.3 Foam and proteinuria

Foam formation when urine contacts water is influenced by surface‑active substances (proteins, bile salts, surfactants) and gas entrainment.
Persistent, fine foam is commonly associated with significant proteinuria in nephrotic‑range protein loss, whereas transient coarse bubbles occur normally.[^42][^47]

In pregnancy:

- Physiologic proteinuria (<300 mg/day) is usually insufficient to generate markedly persistent foam.[^43][^44][^42]
- Pathological proteinuria in preeclampsia or underlying renal disease can produce more obvious foaming, often accompanied by other clinical signs (edema, hypertension, abnormal labs).[^43][^47][^42]

No systematic studies quantify foam persistence as a diagnostic metric in pregnancy, and foam is not included in modern diagnostic criteria for gestational hypertension or preeclampsia.[^45][^47]
For AI applications, foam texture could theoretically serve as a weak surrogate for heavy proteinuria but would be heavily confounded by detergent residues, toilet bowl cleaners, and urine concentration.

### 5.4 Sediment

Visible sediment in urine (particles settling after standing) can include cells, casts, crystals, mucus, and contaminants.
Microscopic studies during pregnancy show that morphotic elements (especially leukocytes) in urinary sediment increase with gestational age, particularly in women with bacteriuria.[^88][^75]

Common visually appreciable sediment types include:

- **Flocculent white/yellow sediment**: often leukocytes, bacteria, or mucus, typical of UTI.
- **Crystalline sediment**: fine sand‑like deposits from urate, phosphate, or calcium salts, influenced by pH and concentration; pregnancy‑related hypercalciuria and alkaline urine may increase calcium phosphate precipitation, but not uniquely to pregnancy.[^64][^65][^66]
- **Red or brown specks**: hematuria or pigment; could arise from stones, infection, or glomerular disease.

Again, sediment patterns are not specific to pregnancy and are dominated by infection and lithiasis status.

## 6. Confounders mimicking pregnancy‑associated urinary changes

A non‑invasive visual or colorimetric pregnancy detection system must robustly handle non‑pregnancy factors that alter urine appearance or composition.
Key confounders include:

### 6.1 Prenatal vitamins and supplements

Prenatal vitamins commonly contain high doses of riboflavin (vitamin B2), other B‑complex vitamins, vitamin C, and iron.
Excess riboflavin is excreted unchanged in urine and has an intense yellow color and fluorescence, producing **bright neon yellow or yellow‑green urine**.[^78][^79][^80][^77][^81]

This color change is well‑documented in general supplement literature and is not specific to pregnancy; anyone taking high‑dose B‑complex or energy drinks with riboflavin can exhibit identical urine color.
Thus, urine color intensity alone is an unreliable marker of pregnancy once vitamin use is common.

Iron and other minerals may darken stool but have minimal direct effects on urine color; some multivitamin excipients can subtly alter odor.

### 6.2 Hydration state

Hydration is a primary determinant of urine color and specific gravity.
Pregnant, lactating, and non‑pregnant women all show strong correlations between urine color score, osmolality, and specific gravity, with color ≥4 indicating under‑hydration.[^55][^54]
Early pregnancy nausea and vomiting, especially HG, can cause dehydration and concentrated dark urine independent of hormonal status.[^50][^51]
Conversely, high fluid intake for wellness or nephrolithiasis prophylaxis produces very pale urine.

For AI models, any pregnancy signal based on subtle chromatic cues must be normalized for estimated urine concentration (e.g., via background calibration or color card) to avoid confounding by hydration.

### 6.3 Diet

Several foods induce dramatic but benign urine color changes:[^82][^83][^84][^81]

- **Beets, blackberries, rhubarb**: pink to red or dark brown urine due to excreted betalain pigments; intensity depends on gastric acidity, gut transit, and variety of beet.
- **Carrots, carrot juice, vitamin C–rich foods or supplements**: orange urine.
- **B‑vitamin–fortified foods and drinks**: fluorescent yellow‑green urine via riboflavin.
- **Asparagus**: greenish tinge and characteristic sulfurous odor in susceptible individuals.

These dietary effects produce color changes that are often more prominent than any pregnancy‑related chromatic shift; they are frequent in the general population and in pregnant women with altered cravings.

### 6.4 Medications

Numerous medications change urine color, including:[^81][^82]

- Phenazopyridine: intense orange to red urine.
- Rifampin: red‑orange.
- Some antacids and cimetidine (Tagamet): bluish tinge.
- Nitrofurantoin: dark yellow to brown.

Pregnant individuals are often treated with antibiotics (e.g., nitrofurantoin) and antiemetics, some of which alter urine appearance.
An AI detector must either identify and exclude such cases or incorporate metadata on medication use.

### 6.5 Menstrual cycle phase in non‑pregnant women

The menstrual cycle influences ovarian steroid secretion and thus urinary E1‑3G and PdG patterns, but these metabolites are not visually apparent.[^31][^30][^29]
However, **macroscopic urine appearance can be altered by menstrual contamination**:

- Mid‑cycle or luteal‑phase spotting and menstrual blood can color urine pink, red, or brown and introduce sediment (clots, endometrial tissue).[^88]
- Vaginal secretions and cervical mucus during ovulation may increase turbidity and visible particulates.

Because early pregnancy often overlaps with the expected time of menses or implantation bleeding, distinguishing menstrual contamination from pregnancy‑related hematuria or discharge is challenging based on images alone.

## 7. Signal strength assessment for smartphone‑based visual detection

This section ranks candidate features by their plausibility as inputs to a non‑invasive, smartphone‑based pregnancy detection system that relies on photographs of urine (with or without dedicated chemical strips).

### 7.1 Native (unstimulated) urine appearance

**High signal:**

- None identified. 
  - There is no robust evidence that pregnancy, in the absence of dehydration, infection, or exogenous factors, produces a consistent, visually detectable change in native urine color, turbidity, foam, or sediment in early gestation.[^49][^70][^54][^47][^55][^42]

**Medium signal:**

- Patterns indirectly related to pregnancy complications (not pregnancy itself):
  - Dark, concentrated urine in the context of persistent vomiting and weight loss may suggest hyperemesis gravidarum, but similar appearances occur with any dehydration.[^51][^54][^50]
  - Cloudy urine with leukocytic sediment and nitrite‑positive dipstick may reflect UTI, which is more common during pregnancy but also prevalent in non‑pregnant women.[^75][^70][^71][^49]

**Low signal:**

- **Color intensity (yellow saturation)**: strongly driven by hydration and riboflavin; pregnancy status is a weak modifier at best.[^79][^77][^54][^55]
- **Turbidity and sediment**: primarily governed by UTI, crystalluria, semen/vaginal contamination; pregnancy modestly changes incidence but not appearance.[^86][^71][^75][^64]
- **Foam**: only clearly increased with heavy proteinuria (e.g., nephrotic‑range), which is uncommon in early pregnancy and not a screening tool.[^47][^42]

**None / negligible signal:**

- Direct visual cues of hCG, estriol, progesterone metabolites, or hPL, since these peptides and steroids are colorless at physiological concentrations.[^34][^30][^1][^8]

### 7.2 Colorimetric strip–based features captured in images

If the system is allowed to interpret **colorimetric urine strips**, signal strength increases substantially.

**High signal:**

- **Dedicated hCG immunochromatographic strips**: existing home tests already achieve high sensitivity and specificity when properly used; smartphone‑aided reading can improve faint‑band detection and timing.[^16][^1][^15]
- **Enzymatic assays for E1‑3G and PdG**: Ovulation/pregnancy monitoring systems that measure urinary estrone conjugates and pregnanediol glucuronide have demonstrated predictive value for conception and luteal sufficiency; a test optimized for peri‑implantation changes could complement hCG in some windows.[^31][^30][^29]

**Medium signal:**

- **Ketone, glucose, and protein pads**: while non‑specific, combinations of ketonuria (HG), mild glucosuria (gestational diabetes or reduced renal threshold), and new‑onset proteinuria could help characterize pregnancy complications rather than detection per se.[^52][^53][^48][^42][^47]
- **pH and crystal surrogates**: elevated pH and concurrent hypercalciuria may indicate gestational stone risk, but pH and crystalluria are not pregnancy‑specific.[^63][^62][^65][^64]

**Low signal:**

- **Leukocyte esterase and nitrites**: useful for detecting UTI, which is common in pregnancy but also in non‑pregnant states; more valuable for safety triage than pregnancy detection.[^74][^72][^73][^71]

## 8. Research gaps and opportunities

Despite extensive work on hCG and steroid metabolism, there are notable gaps relevant to an AI‑based non‑invasive pregnancy detection system.

### 8.1 Quantitative visual signatures

- **Lack of controlled imaging studies**: There are virtually no peer‑reviewed datasets of standardized urine photographs with known pregnancy status, hydration, diet, and medication metadata.
  - Building such datasets would allow supervised learning of whether subtle chromatic, textural, or scattering differences exist between pregnant and non‑pregnant urine beyond obvious confounders.
- **Spectral characterization**: Most work uses human‑perceived color categories; detailed visible/near‑IR absorbance and scattering spectra of early pregnancy urine versus controls are sparse.
  - High‑resolution spectrophotometry and hyperspectral imaging could identify small differences invisible to the human eye but exploitable algorithmically.

### 8.2 hCG isoform and β‑core dynamics in early urine

- **Limited open quantitative data**: Proprietary manufacturer datasets provide qualitative patterns of intact hCG, free β, and β‑core fragment but few open‑access numeric tables across days 6–21 post‑conception.[^13][^11][^12]
  - Additional academic work quantifying each isoform (absolute and creatinine‑normalized) in twice‑daily samples from peri‑implantation onward would refine modeling of earliest detectable signals.
- **Impact on strip performance**: While variant hook effects from β‑core fragment are described, systematic mapping of isoform concentrations versus test band intensity across multiple commercial assays is incomplete.[^10][^25]
  - Such studies would inform AI interpretation of atypical band patterns (e.g., weak test line despite high total hCG).

### 8.3 Non‑hormonal pregnancy fingerprints

- **Crystalluria and lithogenicity**: Gestational hypercalciuria and alkaline urine increase supersaturation, but detailed quantitative studies of crystal type and load in early vs late pregnancy and vs non‑pregnant controls are limited.[^65][^66][^64]
  - Microscopy‑coupled imaging could reveal subtle differences in backscatter or sediment morphology.
- **Sulfur and nitrogen metabolites**: Changes in amino‑acid catabolites, sulfate handling, and one‑carbon metabolism during pregnancy are documented in plasma and urine, but their impact on chromophores or light scattering has not been studied in a visual‑detection context.[^89][^90][^87]

### 8.4 Integration of pregnancy complications

- **Hyperemesis gravidarum**: The relationships among ketonuria, urine concentration, and hormonal milieu in HG are incompletely characterized; recent work questions ketonuria as a severity marker.[^53][^52][^50]
  - Prospective studies including detailed urine chemistry and imaging in HG vs uncomplicated pregnancies could identify secondary visual markers.
- **Early preeclampsia and gestational hypertension**: Early proteinuria, uric acid, and endothelial markers in urine are active research areas, but translation into visually detectable changes (e.g., foam, microbubbles, microcrystals) remains unexplored.

### 8.5 Human urinary relaxin and hPL

- **Urinary relaxin**: While animal work demonstrates robust urinary relaxin signals for early pregnancy diagnosis, comparable human data are minimal.[^38][^37][^36]
  - Development and validation of human urinary relaxin assays (and exploration of any colorimetric coupling reactions) could provide an additional non‑invasive marker.
- **Urinary hPL kinetics**: Older work shows hPL excreted unchanged in urine and suggests potential for viability and dating tests, but modern quantitative reference ranges and assay performance data are lacking.[^35][^34]

### 8.6 Confounder modeling

- **Medication‑induced color shifts in pregnancy**: There is little systematic documentation of how commonly used pregnancy medications (antiemetics, antibiotics, supplements) affect urine appearance in real‑world settings.
- **Cultural and dietary variations**: Diet‑induced pigment excretion is highly variable by region; pregnancy AI models will need locale‑specific priors on the prevalence of beetroot, herbal preparations, and colored beverages.

Addressing these gaps will require coordinated clinical protocols that collect standardized urine samples, high‑fidelity images (including spectral data), detailed biochemical panels (hormones, metabolites, electrolytes, proteins), and metadata on hydration, diet, medications, and reproductive status.
Such multimodal datasets are essential to determine whether native urine appearance can contribute meaningfully—beyond hCG strips—to early pregnancy detection.
